Best Chemical Exfoliants in 2026: AHA, BHA, and PHA Complete Guide

Share on XShare on FacebookShare on LinkedInShare on Reddit

Chemical exfoliation has replaced physical scrubs as the dermatologist-preferred method for removing dead skin cells, unclogging pores, and improving skin texture. Unlike physical exfoliants (scrubs, brushes, washcloths) that work by abrasion and can cause micro-tears in the skin, chemical exfoliants dissolve the bonds between dead skin cells, allowing them to shed naturally. The result is smoother, brighter, more even-toned skin with less risk of irritation or damage when used correctly.

The chemical exfoliant market in 2026 offers more options than ever, and navigating the alphabet soup of AHAs, BHAs, and PHAs can be overwhelming. This guide explains what each type does, which skin concerns it addresses, and which specific products deliver the best results at every price point. Whether you are dealing with acne, hyperpigmentation, fine lines, or rough texture, there is a chemical exfoliant that can help -- but choosing the wrong one can make things worse.

How Chemical Exfoliation Works

Your skin naturally sheds dead cells through a process called desquamation. In healthy young skin, this cycle takes approximately 28 days. As you age, the cycle slows to 35-45 days, leading to a buildup of dead cells that makes skin look dull, rough, and uneven. Chemical exfoliants accelerate this natural process by dissolving the "glue" (desmosomes) that holds dead cells together, allowing them to shed and revealing the fresher, smoother skin underneath.

Chemical exfoliants also stimulate cell turnover deeper in the epidermis, signaling the skin to produce new cells more rapidly. Over time, this results in thicker, healthier skin with improved collagen production and reduced hyperpigmentation. However, these benefits only occur with consistent, long-term use -- typically 8-12 weeks before visible results become apparent.

AHAs (Alpha Hydroxy Acids)

AHAs are water-soluble acids derived from natural sources (sugar cane, milk, fruits) that work on the skin surface. They are the best choice for improving texture, fading hyperpigmentation, reducing the appearance of fine lines, and adding radiance to dull skin.

Glycolic Acid

The smallest AHA molecule, glycolic acid penetrates most deeply and produces the most dramatic results. It is the gold standard for anti-aging exfoliation. Concentrations of 5-10% are effective for home use; higher concentrations (20-70%) are used in professional chemical peels. Glycolic acid increases photosensitivity, so daily sunscreen is mandatory when using it. Best products: The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7% Toning Solution ($9), Paula's Choice 8% AHA Gel Exfoliant ($32).

Lactic Acid

A larger molecule than glycolic acid, lactic acid penetrates less deeply and is therefore gentler. It also has humectant properties, meaning it attracts moisture to the skin while exfoliating. This makes lactic acid the best AHA for dry and sensitive skin types. Best products: The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5% ($7), Sunday Riley Good Genes ($85).

Mandelic Acid

The largest common AHA molecule, mandelic acid penetrates slowly and produces the gentlest exfoliation. It has antibacterial properties that make it particularly effective for acne-prone skin that is also sensitive. Mandelic acid is also one of the safest AHAs for darker skin tones, as its slow penetration reduces the risk of post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation. Best products: The Ordinary Mandelic Acid 10% ($7), Wishtrend Mandelic Acid 5% Skin Prep Water ($22).

BHAs (Beta Hydroxy Acids)

Salicylic acid is the primary BHA used in skincare. Unlike AHAs, which are water-soluble and work on the skin surface, salicylic acid is oil-soluble, allowing it to penetrate into pores and dissolve the sebum and dead skin cells that cause blackheads, whiteheads, and acne. Salicylic acid also has anti-inflammatory properties, reducing the redness and swelling associated with active breakouts.

Concentrations of 0.5-2% are standard for over-the-counter products. The 2% concentration is most common and most effective for acne treatment. Salicylic acid does not increase photosensitivity as significantly as AHAs, but sunscreen is still recommended.

Best products: Paula's Choice Skin Perfecting 2% BHA Liquid Exfoliant ($34 for 4 oz, $12 for travel size), CosRx BHA Blackhead Power Liquid ($22), The Ordinary Salicylic Acid 2% Solution ($6).

PHAs (Polyhydroxy Acids)

PHAs are the newest generation of chemical exfoliants and are the gentlest option available. Common PHAs include gluconolactone and lactobionic acid. Their molecular size is significantly larger than AHAs or BHAs, meaning they do not penetrate as deeply and cause less irritation. PHAs also have humectant and antioxidant properties, providing multiple skin benefits beyond exfoliation.

PHAs are ideal for people with very sensitive skin, rosacea, or eczema who cannot tolerate AHAs or BHAs. They do not increase photosensitivity and can be used alongside retinoids without the irritation that AHA + retinoid combinations can cause.

Best products: Naturium PHA Topical Acid 12% ($15), COSRX PHA Moisture Renewal Power Cream ($24).

How to Use Chemical Exfoliants Safely

  1. Start slowly: Begin with 1-2 applications per week regardless of which exfoliant you choose. Increase frequency only after 2-3 weeks with no irritation.
  2. Apply to clean, dry skin: After cleansing, pat your face dry and wait 1-2 minutes. Apply the exfoliant evenly, avoiding the eye area and lips.
  3. Wait before layering: Allow the exfoliant to absorb for 5-10 minutes before applying moisturizer. This ensures proper pH levels for the acid to work effectively.
  4. Always use sunscreen: AHAs in particular increase photosensitivity. SPF 30+ every morning is non-negotiable when using chemical exfoliants.
  5. Do not combine with retinol: Using AHA/BHA on the same night as retinol can cause excessive irritation. Alternate nights: exfoliant one evening, retinol the next.
  6. Listen to your skin: Mild tingling is normal. Burning, prolonged redness, or peeling means you are over-exfoliating. Reduce frequency or switch to a gentler product.

Common Mistakes

Over-Exfoliation

The most common mistake is using exfoliants too frequently or combining multiple exfoliating products. Signs of over-exfoliation include persistent redness, tightness, flaking, increased breakouts, and skin that stings when you apply moisturizer. If you experience these symptoms, stop all exfoliation for 2-4 weeks and focus on barrier repair with ceramide-rich moisturizers.

Using Physical and Chemical Exfoliants Together

Using a scrub and a chemical exfoliant in the same routine is a recipe for barrier damage. Choose one method, not both. Chemical exfoliation is generally gentler and more effective for most people.

Expecting Overnight Results

Chemical exfoliants work at the cellular level, and visible results typically take 8-12 weeks of consistent use. Some people experience a "purging" phase in the first 2-4 weeks where existing congestion comes to the surface, causing temporary breakouts. This is normal with BHA products and resolves on its own. If breakouts continue beyond 6 weeks, the product may not be right for your skin.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between AHA and BHA?
AHAs are water-soluble and work on the skin surface to improve texture, tone, and fine lines. BHAs are oil-soluble and penetrate into pores to dissolve sebum and dead skin cells. AHAs are best for dry, sun-damaged, or aging skin. BHAs are best for oily, acne-prone skin with clogged pores.
How often should I use chemical exfoliants?
Start with 1-2 times per week and increase to every other day only if tolerated. Most dermatologists recommend 2-3 times per week maximum. Over-exfoliation damages the skin barrier, causing dryness, redness, and paradoxically more breakouts.
Can I use AHA and BHA together?
You can, but proceed with caution. The safest approach is to alternate nights. If you want both in one routine, start with a combined product at lower concentrations like The Ordinary's AHA 30% + BHA 2% Peeling Solution, used once per week maximum.
Do chemical exfoliants increase sun sensitivity?
AHAs increase photosensitivity for up to 7 days after use. You must wear sunscreen daily when using AHA products. BHAs do not increase photosensitivity as significantly. Many dermatologists recommend using AHAs only in the evening routine.
What is the best chemical exfoliant for beginners?
Lactic acid at 5-10% is the gentlest starting point. It exfoliates while hydrating, making it suitable for sensitive and dry skin. The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5% ($7) is an excellent beginner product. Graduate to glycolic acid or salicylic acid based on your specific concerns.
